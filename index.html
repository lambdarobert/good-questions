How to get your questions answered faster.

This is a list of tips on how to get questions answered more quickly in online spaces. It's tailored toward technical questions in asynchronous media such as emails, chat rooms, and forums.

If you want an overarching theme to remember, the rule of thumb is to give the person answering as little needless work as possible, so they'll be incentivized to look at your question sooner. This is a win-win for both of you— you get an answer faster; they can answer it more easily.

Be Upfront— Minimize Back-and-Forth

Hey, any JavaScript pros around that could help me?
I am trying to do X, Y, and Z with JavaScript. Here's what I've tried so far and here's why it failed:

Add as much information as you can to your initial post when you ask a question. This will make it easier to answer because the answerer won't have to ask as many clarifying questions.

In general, when communicating over an asynchronous medium, people prefer answering questions on their own time over carving out a specific block of time to help someone. As a result, they'll try to avoid questions that involve a lot of back-and-forth.

"No Hello"

Hi!
Hi! I ran into this problem with X the other day and needed some help with...

Another tempting mistake is to start with a greeting like "Hi!" or "Hello!" This can key people into the fact that someone's online and may need help, but if you then spend several minutes writing a longer response while others' attention is on you, they might lose interest and go back to what they were doing before. On the other hand, if you include the problem you want solved in your initial message, they'll see it immediately and might help.

State Your Problem, Not Just Your Solution

How do I do X?
How do I do X? I'm trying to use it to solve PROBLEM.

This is a restatement of the infamous "XY Problem"— the case where someone is trying to get help using X to solve their problem, when in reality the solution to their problem is Y. If you ask about your problem (and not just the way you intend to solve it), others can provide feedback on possible alternative solutions that could work better or be easier to implement, saving you lots of time and effort.

Ask Where to find Things

How do I do X?
Can you send me some articles about how to do X?

You'll probably get a faster answer to your questions if you ask about where you can find an answer rather than for an answer directly— From an answerer's POV, making a personalized response to a question is a lot harder than just sending a link or two.

Ask for Asynchronous Communication (if possible)

I need help with X! Can someone join a voice call with me?
I need help with X! Fine if you respond here.

If you are asking for help in an asynchronous medium, (one where people don't necessarily have to communicate in real time— e.g. email, a chat room, forums) then it's best to keep communication asynchronous. Trying to move communication to a synchronous, real-time medium (e.g. a voice/video call) will likely be a turn-off for potential answerers because it means they have to carve out a block of time where they are dedicated on answering your question. On the other hand, in an asynchronous medium, they are free to reply whenever they have spare time.

This isn't to say that synchronous communication is bad, however— many will still be willing to join a voice or video call. The main issue is that fewer people willl.

Not Everyone Knows What You Don't Know

I already know X!
The part I'm more confused about is Y.

Remember that the people helping you likely won't know what aspects of the problem you're familiar with— and those you aren't familiar with. Thus, you'll often run into cases where you're told things you already know. While it might seem tempting to get defensive, remember that the person helping you isn't doing this to talk down to you— they just don't know what you already know. The most diplomatic way to handle a situation like this is to redirect the conversation to what you don't know.
